Step 1

Document Everything

Step 1: Document Everything

This step is probably the most important, and almost everyone skips it.

The Reality Check

We had a client who thought they had a simple 6-step process. When we actually sat down and documented it, it turned out to be a 47-step nightmare that none of their employees could fully follow.

What Good Documentation Looks Like

Think of it this way: write it so a 5-year-old could follow it and a 95-year-old could understand it. That means documenting:

⚡
Every login (which
system, which credentials, which screen)
⚡
Every decision point
(if X happens, do Y; if Z happens, do W)
⚡
Every copy and
paste (from where, to where, in what format)
⚡
Every check or
verification (what are you looking for, what counts as...
⚡
Every handoff (who
does it go to next, how do they receive it)
📋 Template
Template: Process Documentation
Field What to Write
Process Name e.g., "New Client Onboarding"
Department e.g., Operations
Owner Who's responsible for this process
Trigger What kicks off this process
Steps Numbered, detailed, every single action
Tools Used List every software involved
Decision Points Where does a human need to make a judgement call
Output What's the final deliverable
Handoff Who receives the output and what do they do with it
Time to Complete How long does this take on average
Frequency How often is this done (daily, weekly, per client)
Common Mistakes Where do people typically screw this up
Example: Documenting an Email Response Process
❌ Bad
Bad documentation: "Check emails and reply to customers."
✅ Good
Good documentation:
  1. Log into Gmail (operations@company.com)
  2. Open inbox and filter by "Unread"
  3. For each email, check if the sender exists in the CRM (HubSpot)
  4. If they exist: open their contact record, note their account status
  5. If they don't exist: create a new contact with name, email, company
  6. Read the email and categorise it (support request, sales enquiry, billing question, general)
  7. For support requests: check if there's an existing ticket in Zendesk
  8. If ticket exists: add the email as a note to the existing ticket
  9. If no ticket: create a new ticket with priority level based on account tier
  10. Draft a reply using the appropriate template from the template library
  11. Customise the template with the client's specific details
  12. Send the reply
  13. Log the interaction in HubSpot under the contact record
  14. Update the ticket status in Zendesk
See the difference? The second version is what an AI can actually work with.
How Many Processes Should You Document?

Start with your top 10 most time-consuming processes. Don't try to document everything at once.

🔑

Key Takeaway: If you can't document it, you can't automate it. This step alone — even without any AI — will reveal inefficiencies you didn't know existed.

Step 5: Deploy Without the Mess

You've documented your processes, audited your time, architected your solution, and built it. Now you have to actually put it into your business.

1. Quality Assurance (QA): Human vs. AI

Quality assurance means making sure the AI's output is correct before it goes anywhere important.

The rule of thumb is simple:

If the output goes to a human or an external platform → a human must QA it.

Examples:

⚡
AI writes an
email to a client → human reviews before sending
⚡
AI generates a
safety compliance report → human checks before filing
⚡
AI creates a
proposal for a prospect → human approves before delivery
📋 Template
The Two-Layer QA System

We recommend building two layers of quality assurance into every workflow:

This two-layer system reduces mistakes by magnitudes compared to manual-only processes. In many cases, the AI QA layer catches errors that humans would have missed.

Layer Who What They Do Catches
Layer 1: AI QA Built into the workflow Checks output against rules, templates, and expected formats 90% of issues
Layer 2: Human QA Department manager or team lead Reviews flagged items and random samples The remaining 10% (edge cases, nuance, judgement calls)

Watch Out

Common Mistakes

Common Mistakes That Cost Businesses Thousands

We've seen these mistakes hundreds of times. Don't make them.

Mistake 1: Skipping Documentation

"We know our processes, we don't need to write them down." No, you don't. You think you do.

Mistake 2: Automating the Wrong Things First

Don't automate the cool stuff first. Automate the boring, repetitive, time-consuming stuff. The tasks nobody wants to do. That's where the ROI is.

Mistake 3: No Quality Assurance

"The AI is smart enough, we don't need to check its work." Wrong. AI makes mistakes. Confidently. Without QA, one bad output going to a client can cost you more than the entire automation saved.

Mistake 4: Trying to Do Everything at Once

Deploy one workflow at a time. Get it stable. Train the team. Then move to the next one. Companies that try to automate everything simultaneously end up with nothing working properly.

Mistake 5: Treating AI Like a Magic Button

AI is a tool, not a miracle. It needs good inputs (your documented SOPs), clear instructions (your architecture), and oversight (your QA process). Garbage in, garbage out still applies.

Mistake 6: Ignoring Your Team

Your employees will resist change if they feel threatened by it. Frame AI as a way to remove the boring parts of their job, not as a replacement for their role.

Mistake 7: Not Tracking ROI

If you're not measuring what you're saving, how do you know it's working? Track time saved, errors reduced, and capacity gained. Every month. No exceptions.
